# SillyRPG — Game Design Document v2

## 1. High Concept

**SillyRPG** is a narrative-driven life sim / thriller / horror / drama / detective game set in a divided industrial city hiding the truth about a catastrophic chemical disaster.

The player controls a protagonist who returns to his hometown after five years away. He initially plans to stay only a few days, but becomes trapped in a web of disappearances, lies, decaying memories, and the living consequences of a disaster that may be tied directly to him.

At first, the protagonist appears to be a victim — betrayed, traumatized, broken. Over time, it becomes clear that he may have played a terrible role in the city’s collapse, and that both his memory and the city’s official history have been deliberately distorted.

The game combines:
- **Life sim** — daily routine, movement around the city, finances, sleep, resources, relationships.
- **Detective investigation** — collecting evidence, comparing testimonies, reconstructing timelines, building theories.
- **Psychological horror** — fractured memory, dreams, flashbacks, dissociation, urban decay, unreliable perception.
- **Survival pressure** — night excursions into the abandoned zone, dangerous encounters, limited resources.
- **Tactical combat** — dirty, brutal, low-fantasy street fights inspired by old-school browser combat systems.

---

## 2. Core Premise

Five years ago, a catastrophic accident at the city’s chemical plant destroyed part of the city and poisoned the old industrial district.

The protagonist was working that night.

Shortly before the accident, he discovered his wife’s infidelity. Emotionally shattered, he drank while on shift. During a critical systems failure, he failed to respond in time. Safety systems, already compromised by corruption and cost-cutting, did not function correctly. He tried to contain the disaster manually, descended into the lower levels of the plant, and survived the explosion only by accident. A head injury caused severe trauma and fragmented memory.

When he woke up in the hospital, nobody was there:
- his friends were dead,
- his wife believed he may have done it on purpose,
- the city had already begun constructing an official lie.

Now he returns.

He finds a city split into two halves:
- a **new city**, built beside the ruins and sustained by denial,
- an **old city**, half-dead, poisoned, cordoned off, and full of traces the authorities never wanted found.

The deeper he investigates, the more he learns:
- the dead were hidden, miscounted, or reclassified as missing over the years,
- old friends did not disappear recently — many died long ago,
- the city covered up the true scale of the catastrophe,
- his wife concealed part of the truth,
- and he himself may be far more responsible than he believed.

## 3. Pillars

### 3.1 Life Sim Infected by Horror
At first, the player performs ordinary actions: sleeping, eating, traveling, meeting people, sorting documents, managing money. Gradually, these routines become infected by tension, memory collapse, fear, and moral pressure.

### 3.2 The City as a Psychological Mirror
The city is not just a setting. It reflects the protagonist’s state of mind:
- the **new city** represents repression, reconstruction, official lies, and social masks,
- the **old city** represents guilt, truth, decay, trauma, and memory.

### 3.3 Shared Guilt
The protagonist is not the only guilty party. The disaster was the result of a chain:
- personal collapse,
- managerial corruption,
- unsafe plant operations,
- political cover-up,
- collective denial.

### 3.4 Investigation Through Unreliable Memory
The player is not merely searching for clues; they are reconstructing reality through contradictory evidence, damaged memory, and manipulated narratives.

### 3.5 Violence as Urban Pressure
Combat and random encounters are not power fantasy. Violence is dirty, stressful, and costly. The city itself pushes back against the player.

## 4. Setting Overview

### 4.1 The New City
The new city is clean, functional, and outwardly civilized. It contains:
- apartment blocks and new residential districts,
- municipal buildings,
- hospital,
- archives,
- bars and cafés,
- memorial spaces,
- offices and corporate spaces,
- transport and service infrastructure.

Atmosphere:
- artificial normalcy,
- civic slogans about recovery and rebirth,
- polished surfaces covering unresolved rot,
- citizens who avoid certain topics,
- tension beneath politeness.

### 4.2 The Old City
The old city is the abandoned, poisoned, half-dead part of town. It contains:
- ruined residential courtyards,
- schools, kindergartens, clinics,
- empty stores,
- industrial outskirts,
- underground maintenance tunnels,
- rail lines and utility corridors,
- the destroyed chemical plant at the edge of the zone.

Atmosphere:
- life interrupted and never resumed,
- familiar places twisted by time and contamination,
- silence, rust, dust, dampness,
- evidence of hurried evacuation,
- constant sense that the district should have been buried, not preserved.

### 4.3 Official and Unofficial Reality
Officially, the old city is dangerous because of:
- chemical contamination,
- structural instability,
- residual toxicity,
- sanitary restrictions.

In reality, it is also restricted because it contains:
- traces of the true scale of the disaster,
- hidden evidence,
- forgotten dead,
- destroyed records that were never fully destroyed,
- proof of long-term corruption.

## 5. Old Zone / Night Excursion Layer

The old city becomes a dedicated gameplay layer explored primarily at night.

### 5.1 Why Night Matters
During the day:
- military patrols and cordon checkpoints are active,
- access is restricted,
- certain routes are watched,
- official movement is harder to hide.

At night:
- the patrol presence weakens,
- some checkpoints are poorly manned or abandoned,
- smugglers and scavengers move through breaches,
- the zone becomes more dangerous for everyone.

This creates the central rhythm:
- **Day** — social life, investigation, preparation, planning.
- **Night** — infiltration, exploration, confrontation, discovery, survival.

### 5.2 Why Patrols Avoid the Zone at Night
Rational explanation:
- poor visibility,
- unstable structures,
- pockets of toxic fog,
- radio interference,
- animal attacks,
- previous disappearances among patrols.

Urban folklore explanation:
- “the old city wakes up at night,”
- “if you hear the siren, don’t turn around,”
- “the plant still breathes after midnight,”
- “the dead who were never counted still walk there.”

## 6. Narrative Structure

### Act 1 — Return
The protagonist comes back to town for what should be a short stay, likely tied to a funeral or another unavoidable event.

He begins to notice:
- strange reactions from locals,
- gaps in his own memory,
- old photos containing faces he cannot place,
- mentions of people who “went missing,”
- the city’s split personality.

### Act 2 — Investigation
He starts pursuing leads:
- old friends,
- plant workers,
- family history,
- public records,
- rumors about disappearances,
- inconsistencies in death registries,
- encounters with his wife.

Fragments of the past begin to return:
- the affair,
- the shift,
- the control room,
- the alarms,
- the descent into the lower levels.

### Act 3 — False Truth
The player may initially conclude that the catastrophe was entirely caused by corruption, the wife, executives, investors, or political actors.

This is only partially true.

### Act 4 — Real Truth
The player learns:
- the protagonist was drunk on shift,
- he missed a critical window,
- the plant was already a disaster waiting to happen,
- the wife helped bury the truth,
- the city dispersed the real death toll over years,
- many “missing people” were disaster victims all along,
- the new city was built over a social lie.

### Act 5 — Catharsis
The ending revolves around truth, guilt, and the cost of exposing reality.
Possible ending directions:
- public confession,
- private confrontation with the wife,
- exposing the city and destroying what remains of its false stability,
- choosing between justice and survival.

## 7. Core Gameplay Loop

A standard daily loop:
1. Wake up / recover / deal with consequences of previous day.
2. Plan the day and review current leads.
3. Travel through the city.
4. Handle daytime activities:
   - meetings,
   - errands,
   - archives,
   - social visits,
   - earning or spending resources,
   - preparing for a night run.
5. Night phase:
   - sleep,
   - dream event,
   - or enter the old city for a risky excursion.
6. Process consequences:
   - new clues,
   - injuries,
   - memory triggers,
   - reputation shifts,
   - changes in availability of people and places.

## 8. Life Sim Systems

### 8.1 Housing / Safe Space
The protagonist has a temporary living space:
- inherited apartment,
- rented room,
- relative’s house,
- similar low-comfort base.

Functions:
- sleep and recovery,
- evidence sorting,
- inventory storage,
- reflection scenes,
- dream sequences,
- a visual indicator of the protagonist’s mental deterioration or stabilization.

### 8.2 Money and Resources
Resources should matter but not become tedious.
Examples:
- food,
- transport / fuel,
- medicine,
- alcohol,
- cigarettes,
- bribes,
- gear for old-zone runs,
- archive and document fees.

### 8.3 Schedule and Availability
The city follows schedules:
- archives are open by day,
- bars are active in the evening,
- some people only speak at certain times,
- restricted locations have windows of opportunity,
- key events can be missed if the player delays too long.

## 9. Psychological State Systems

The protagonist’s condition is represented through multiple interlinked variables.

### 9.1 Fatigue
Affects:
- perception,
- dialogue quality,
- success in tense situations,
- vulnerability to breakdowns,
- dream intensity.

### 9.2 Stress
Affects:
- impulse control,
- available dialogue responses,
- combat consistency,
- likelihood of panic events,
- frequency and severity of flashbacks.

### 9.3 Clarity
Represents mental sharpness and ability to process reality.
Affects:
- quality of deductions,
- memory reconstruction,
- noticing inconsistencies,
- reading people.

### 9.4 Suppression
Represents how much the protagonist is shutting down emotionally.
High suppression may:
- reduce immediate panic,
- weaken some emotional reactions,
- delay or block important memories,
- make relationships feel colder and more alien.

### 9.5 Alcohol Craving
A major tension mechanic.
Alcohol can:
- reduce short-term stress,
- make entering traumatic places easier,
- help the protagonist sleep.

But it also:
- damages clarity,
- worsens memory reconstruction,
- sabotages conversations,
- increases the risk of repeating old patterns,
- can destabilize combat and night exploration.

---

## 10. Memory as a Core Mechanic

Memory is not delivered only through cutscenes. It is an active system.

### 10.1 Memory Triggers
Fragments can be unlocked through:
- locations,
- smells,
- sounds,
- objects,
- photographs,
- dialogue,
- injuries,
- dreams,
- artifacts from the old zone.

### 10.2 Fragment Structure
Recovered memory comes in fragments, not complete scenes.
Examples:
- a red alarm panel,
- a hand holding a glass,
- part of an argument,
- a bloodied stairwell,
- a maintenance corridor,
- a specific song or phrase.

### 10.3 False Interpretation
The player can build incorrect conclusions from incomplete memory.
The game should support ambiguity until stronger evidence is found.

## 11. Investigation Systems

### 11.1 Clues and Leads
The player gathers:
- documents,
- testimonies,
- official records,
- photographs,
- location evidence,
- artifact-linked impressions,
- personal notes,
- recovered memories.

### 11.2 Theory Board / Case Web
The player uses a board, notebook, or similar interface to connect:
- people,
- places,
- dates,
- events,
- contradictory claims,
- memory fragments,
- physical evidence.

The point is not just collection, but hypothesis-building.

### 11.3 Version-Based Progression
Progress is driven by forming and testing narrative versions:
- who died when,
- who lied,
- who benefited,
- what failed at the plant,
- whether the protagonist was set up,
- how much guilt belongs to him.

Some theories are later disproven or transformed.

## 12. Social Systems

Important NPCs are not static quest dispensers. Each major character operates through relational variables.

### Possible variables
- trust,
- fear,
- guilt,
- exhaustion,
- loyalty to the official narrative,
- residual affection,
- resentment.

### Dialogue approaches
The player may:
- ask gently,
- press hard,
- confront with evidence,
- lie,
- manipulate,
- reminisce,
- provoke,
- seek comfort,
- threaten.

Conversations can:
- open leads,
- close doors,
- trigger disappearances,
- alter reputation,
- cause people to warn others,
- produce partial truth.

---

## 13. The Wife

The wife is one of the core narrative axes of the game.

She is not a simple villain. She is:
- the CEO or public face of the company that owned the plant,
- a person who loved the protagonist,
- someone who betrayed him,
- someone who may believe he destroyed the city in a moment of rage,
- someone whose own fear and guilt helped sustain the lie.

Her relationship system may involve:
- residual love,
- anger,
- grief,
- shame,
- fear of truth,
- desire to preserve the official order,
- willingness to protect or obstruct the protagonist.

Each meeting with her should feel heavy, personal, and dangerous.

## 14. Random Travel Events

Travel across the city is not a passive transition. Movement can trigger events.

### Event categories
#### Social
- chance encounter with an old acquaintance,
- overheard rumor,
- someone recognizing the protagonist,
- a spontaneous lead,
- unexpected request for help.

#### Dangerous
- mugging,
- harassment,
- intimidation,
- fight in a bar or alley,
- ambush,
- people trying to stop the protagonist from digging.

#### Psychological
- panic attack,
- dissociation episode,
- false recognition,
- flashback in transit,
- lost time.

These events should depend on:
- district,
- time of day,
- protagonist state,
- reputation,
- story progression,
- current inventory / evidence,
- whether the protagonist is intoxicated.

## 15. Combat System

Combat should evoke old browser fighting games, especially classic zone-based combat, while remaining grounded and thematically appropriate.

### 15.1 Combat Tone
Combat is:
- brutal,
- ugly,
- stressful,
- costly,
- short.

It is not heroic spectacle.

### 15.2 Structure
Combat is turn-based or round-based.
In each round, the player chooses:
- attack target zone,
- defense zone(s),
- special action.

Possible zones:
- head,
- torso,
- stomach,
- legs.

Possible actions:
- strike,
- block,
- dodge,
- pressure,
- retreat,
- dirty move,
- item usage.

### 15.3 Combat Influences
Combat outcome depends on:
- character stats,
- fatigue,
- stress,
- injuries,
- intoxication,
- weapon or improvised object,
- enemy behavior pattern.

### 15.4 Consequences
After combat the player may suffer:
- bruises,
- fractures,
- concussion,
- limping,
- pain penalties,
- item loss,
- reputation effects,
- memory triggers.

### 15.5 Enemy Types
Potential encounter types:
- local thugs,
- drunk aggressors,
- hired intimidators,
- security staff,
- desperate scavengers,
- zone raiders,
- emotionally loaded opponents linked to the disaster.

## 16. The Old Zone Ecology

The old zone should not feel like generic post-apocalypse. Its horrors emerge from industrial poisoning, time, abandonment, and rumor.

### 16.1 Mutation Lore
The plant released a mixture of:
- industrial reagents,
- heavy metals,
- combustion products,
- experimental catalysts,
- unreported compounds never acknowledged in official reports.

This reshaped the local ecosystem:
- some life forms died out,
- some adapted,
- some became physically or behaviorally distorted.

### 16.2 Mutated Fauna
#### Feral scavenger dogs
- hairless patches,
- diseased skin,
- malformed jaws,
- reduced pain response,
- pack aggression.

#### Rat colonies
- dense underground infestations,
- aggressive swarming,
- contamination spread.

#### Corrupted birds
- abnormal flocking,
- territorial aggression,
- attraction to bright objects and remains,
- use as environmental omen.

#### Insects
Examples:
- translucent, chemically altered beetles or flies,
- swarming insects in hot contaminated pockets,
- nests in vents, basements, and damp ruins.

### 16.3 Human Degradation
Rather than simple “monsters,” some humans in the zone are:
- scavengers who never left,
- abandoned workers,
- addicts,
- outcasts,
- heavily contaminated survivors.

Their transformation may be physical, neurological, social, or ambiguous.

Rare extreme cases may look almost inhuman — but the game should preserve uncertainty where possible.

---

## 17. Marauders / Zone Economy

The old zone has produced its own underground economy.

Groups may include:
- scrap hunters,
- illegal artifact seekers,
- smugglers,
- former plant workers,
- ex-security personnel,
- desperate residents from the new city.

They enter the zone for:
- metal,
- black-market materials,
- hidden valuables,
- plant documentation,
- chemical remnants,
- artifacts.

This creates conflict, territory, and violence.

## 18. Anomalies and Artifacts

### 18.1 Artifact Concept
Artifacts are strange objects formed by the disaster and the altered environment.
They are not fully magical in a fantasy sense. They are ambiguous products of chemistry, heat, pressure, contamination, and myth.

Officially, they are dismissed as hazardous debris.
Unofficially, they are:
- traded,
- feared,
- collected,
- used by scavengers,
- studied by fringe researchers,
- treated as charms or tools.

### 18.2 Artifact Categories
#### Physical artifacts
Examples:
- glassy tear-shaped formations,
- mineralized nodules from pipes and basements,
- porous black slag stones from plant outskirts.

Possible effects:
- mild environmental protection,
- stress reduction,
- resource value,
- hidden toxicity.

#### Psychoactive artifacts
Objects that affect perception and memory:
- sharpen awareness,
- trigger flashbacks,
- amplify anxiety,
- distort sound,
- worsen dreams.

These are especially important for the protagonist.

#### Trade / social artifacts
Valuable primarily because certain factions want them.
They can be used for:
- trade,
- access,
- side quests,
- influence.

### 18.3 Artifacts and Memory
Some artifacts resonate with the protagonist’s fractured memory.
They may unlock:
- unique flashbacks,
- alternate interpretations of scenes,
- hidden clues tied to the disaster,
- unstable but powerful clarity boosts.

Cost:
- stress,
- insomnia,
- worsening dissociation,
- alcohol craving.

### 18.4 Environmental Anomalies
Potential old-zone anomalies:
- toxic ground fog,
- humming interference fields,
- warm chemical pools,
- sound corridors that mimic sirens,
- places where light and electronics behave strangely.

These serve both atmosphere and gameplay.

## 19. Progression Philosophy

The player should not simply level up into a stronger hero.
Progression is mainly about:
- understanding,
- resilience,
- access,
- social positioning,
- survival preparedness,
- tolerance for truth.

Possible progression vectors:
- improved ability to survive the old zone,
- wider access to hidden districts and factions,
- stronger combat options,
- better emotional control,
- expanded memory reconstruction,
- more refined deduction tools.

## 20. Day / Night Split as Genre Engine

This is one of the game’s defining structures.

### Day
- life sim,
- social investigation,
- administration,
- meetings,
- archives,
- city politics,
- preparation.

### Night
- horror exploration,
- zone infiltration,
- dangerous encounters,
- artifact hunting,
- flashback triggers,
- plant approach,
- confrontation with the buried city.

The player should feel that every day is a preparation for an emotional or physical descent at night.

---

## 21. Tone and Emotional Goal

The game should create a feeling of:
- returning to a place that remembers more than you do,
- suspecting everyone,
- living inside half-truths,
- performing ordinary routines while standing above mass death,
- discovering that both you and the city are built on repression,
- realizing that truth may destroy what little remains.

The desired ending emotion is not triumph, but **catharsis through unbearable recognition**.
